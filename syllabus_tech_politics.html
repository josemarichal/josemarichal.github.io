About the Instructor

Dr. Marichal is a Professor of Political Science at California Lutheran University. He studies the role that social media plays in restructuring political behavior and institutions. In 2012, his book Facebook Democracy (Routledge Press) was published: it looks at the ways in which social media platforms encourage antagonistic political discourse and how they could be regulated. In addition, Dr. Marichal organized the Western Political Science Association’s mini-conference on Algorithmic Politics. Currently, he is working on a book focused on the damaging effects among citizens of “algorithmic mentality” on democracy.

Course Description

This course will examine the role that data collection (e.g. social media apps or Alexa personal assistants) and analysis tools (machine learning and artificial intelligence algorithms) play in our daily lives and upon social/political/economic institutions. This course will draw from a broad range of disciplines (Political Science, Information Science, Sociology, Law, Communication, Philosophy, Psychology, etc.) to explore these questions.

Course Assignments & Assessment

Leading Deliberative Discussion (15 x 1 = 15 points): Twice during the semester, you will be part of a group that leads discussion. We will divide the class into groups, assigning each group a specific AI-related ethical dilemma (e.g., facial recognition, autonomous vehicles). Groups of three will be assigned to lead discussion and present arguments for and against the ethical implications of their assigned topic. This will be structured less as a debate and more of an exploration of diverse ideas on the topic with the ultimate goal of engaging in constructive discussions and work towards a consensus on how to address the ethical concerns. During these sessions, we will explore elements of effective argumentation and evidence evaluation, aspects of critical thinking and logical reasoning and how to craft a well constructed argument. Other students will be play the role of devil’s advocate.

Exams (2 exams x 25 points = 50 points): Because this is a writing intensive course, you’ll be asked to write. You will have two take home essay exams in which you will be asked to apply the theories and concepts learned to specific questions. At the end of our final section of the course, we will do a second exam that will have the same objectives. Each essay exam will be worth 25 points.

Debate (25 points): At the end of the the semester, we will have a structured debate in groups.. where each person will be assigned to represent different constituencies on digital privacy (technology companies, government agencies, cybersecurity experts, and privacy advocates). The assignment will be broken down into three sections:

  • Identifying reliable sources. You will provide me with 7 sources you will use in your debate.
  • Argument analysis. You will produce a rough draft of five major arguments.
  • Final statement. You will be required to prepare a brief statement outlining your assigned constituency’s viewpoint and supporting evidence. This statement will be prepared three weeks before the assignment and distributed to me and the other members of your group that will provide peer feedback.

Case Study Analysis Portfolio (10 points): Each Friday, you will be assigned a case study based on the week’s readings. An example might be the topic of data security breaches in various industries (e.g., Equifax, Facebook). You will be asked to identify the causes, consequences, and the ethical considerations involved and to come prepared to have a group discussion where you propose alternative strategies to address the case. You will be asked to role play multiple different constituencies on digital privacy (technology companies, government agencies, cybersecurity experts, and privacy advocates). You will write a reflection on your participation upon the case study assignments to the Canvas discussion at the end of class.
