About the Instructor

Dr. Marichal is a Professor of Political Science at California Lutheran University. He studies the role that social media plays in restructuring political behavior and institutions. In 2012, his book Facebook Democracy (Routledge Press) was published: it looks at the ways in which social media platforms encourage antagonistic political discourse and how they could be regulated. In addition, Dr. Marichal organized the Western Political Science Association’s mini-conference on Algorithmic Politics. Currently, he is working on a book focused on the damaging effects among citizens of “algorithmic mentality” on democracy.

Course Description

This course will examine the role that data collection (e.g. social media apps or Alexa personal assistants) and analysis tools (machine learning and artificial intelligence algorithms) play in our daily lives and upon social/political/economic institutions. This course will draw from a broad range of disciplines (Political Science, Information Science, Sociology, Law, Communication, Philosophy, Psychology, etc.) to explore these questions.

Course Assignments: Technology & Politics (Spring 2026)

Total Points: 100

  • Part 1: The "Machine Liberalism" Reflexive Journal (60 Points)
  • Part 2: The Capstone Project: The Campus Listening Experiment (40 Points)

Part 1: The "Machine Liberalism" Reflexive Journal (60 Points)

Objective: To connect the theoretical concepts of each Unit to the real-world implications for democratic life.

Format: For each of the 6 Units, you will submit a 2–3 page reflection paper. This is not a summary of the readings. It is an application of the theory.

The Prompt: "Based on the readings and guest seminars from Unit [X], identify one specific way that algorithmic logic (e.g., Optimization, Preemption, Branding) distorts how citizens speak or how governments listen. If we are moving from a 'Liberal Subject' to a 'Digital Subject,' what is gained and what is lost in this specific domain?"

Grading Criteria (10 Points Each):

  • Synthesis (4 pts): Does it meaningfully connect at least two readings or guest perspectives?
  • Application (4 pts): Does it apply the theory to a concrete example (a law, app, or event)?
  • Clarity (2 pts): Is the argument sharp and well-structured?

Due Dates (Mondays @ 11:59pm):

  • Unit 1 (Theory): Due Monday, Feb 16
  • Unit 2 (Economy): Due Monday, Mar 16
  • Unit 3 (Epistemic): Due Monday, Apr 13
  • Unit 4 (Security): Due Monday, May 4
  • Unit 5 (Social): Due Monday, May 11
  • Unit 6 (Resistance): Due Wednesday, May 13 (with Final Audit)

Part 2: Capstone Project (40 Points)

"The Campus Listening Experiment: A Participatory AI Lab Simulation"

Format: Group Project (3–4 Students per Team)

Objective: In this capstone, you will act as a "Participatory AI Lab" research team. Your goal is to use AI to "listen" to student sentiment regarding a contentious campus issue. You will generate raw data (via survey), process it using Machine Learning (BERTopic), and then audit the results to determine if the algorithm captured the "truth" or merely optimized the "average."

Phase 1: Data Generation (10 Points)

Due: Wednesday, Apr 1

  • Step A: Select a Topic. Pick a campus issue that generates "thick" discourse and affects student life (e.g., The cost of meal plans, Parking enforcement, Campus safety/policing, Academic Freedom, or DEI initiatives).
  • Step B: The Survey. Design a Qualtrics survey to capture student sentiment.
    Constraint: You must include at least two Open-Ended Questions (e.g., "Describe a specific experience you had with..." or "Why do you feel this way?"). We need text data to analyze, not just multiple-choice statistics.
  • Step C: Collection. Distribute the survey to your peers.
    Target: Minimum 30–50 responses per group to generate a sufficient corpus for analysis.
  • Deliverable: A raw .csv file of your survey responses submitted to Canvas.

Phase 2: The Machine Analysis (15 Points)

Due: Wednesday, Apr 22

  • Step A: The Pipeline. Using the Google Colab notebook provided in class, run your raw text data through the BERTopic model.
  • Step B: The Output. Generate the following artifacts:
    • Topic Clusters: A list of the Top 5 themes the AI identified.
    • Visualization: An "Intertopic Distance Map" showing how the ideas relate spatially.
  • Step C: The "Civic Insight Brief." Write a 2-page report summarizing what the AI thinks the students care about. Write this as if you were a consultant delivering a "neutral" summary to the University Administration. (e.g., "The AI identified 'Cost' as the primary cluster, with 'Quality' as a distant second.")
  • Deliverable: The Colab Notebook output + The 2-Page Brief.

Phase 3: The Human Audit & Reflection (15 Points)

Due: Wednesday, May 13 (Finals Week)

  • Step A: The "Human Essentiality" Audit. Compare your Raw Data (Phase 1) to the Machine Output (Phase 2). Read the actual comments that the AI summarized.
  • Step B: The Reflection Paper (3–4 Pages). Answer the following questions using the course theory:
    • The Erasure Audit: Who got optimized away? Look at the "outlier" comments that didn't fit into a cluster. Did the AI miss sarcasm, emotion, or minority viewpoints? (Reference Unit 3: The Epistemic Shift).
    • The "Vibes" Check: Did the AI capture the intensity (Thymos) of the student sentiment, or just the keywords? Does the summary feel "true"?
    • The Policy Recommendation: Based on this experiment, write a "Best Practices" statement. Should the University use AI to summarize student feedback? If so, what human guardrails are needed to preserve the "Ontological Integrity" of the student voice?
  • Deliverable: Final Reflection Paper + Group Presentation Slides (for the Final Panel).
