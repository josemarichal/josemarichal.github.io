Living Rights Portfolio

Global Politics in Everyday Life

Assignment Overview

Global politics often feels abstract—something that happens in distant places, at high levels of power, or in institutions students never directly encounter. This assignment is designed to reverse that perspective. The Living Rights Portfolio asks you to explore how global political ideas—power, freedom, security, inequality, democracy, capitalism, technology, and belonging—are lived, felt, and negotiated in your everyday environment.

Rather than studying rights only as laws, treaties, or formal guarantees, this assignment focuses on living rights: the practical ability to speak, move, participate, belong, access resources, and feel secure in daily life. Living rights are not always written down. They emerge through norms, rules, infrastructures, technologies, and power relations—often quietly and unevenly.

Throughout the semester, we will connect major global theories and debates to concrete experiences in spaces you already inhabit. The goal is not to evaluate whether your environment is “good” or “bad,” but to understand how political power operates at close range.

Structure of the Assignment

Beginning in mid-February, most Fridays will be devoted to a Living Rights exercise connected to that week’s readings. Each exercise asks you to observe, analyze, and reflect on how a particular political theme appears in everyday life.

Examples of living rights themes include:

  • Access to resources and support
  • Participation and voice
  • Security and surveillance
  • Belonging and exclusion
  • Technology and control
  • Sustainability and infrastructure
  • Knowledge, transparency, and information

Each Friday activity will be grounded in the theoretical material you have already encountered that week. For example, discussions of capitalism may lead to examining how resources are distributed; discussions of democracy may lead to examining how decisions are made and who participates; discussions of AI may lead to examining how technology shapes opportunity and constraint.

Weekly Portfolio Reflections & "AI-Proofing" Rules

After each Living Rights exercise, you will submit a brief written reflection (approximately 300–400 words) to your portfolio. To ensure this work reflects your unique, physical presence and cannot be simulated by AI, you must adhere to the following three constraints for every entry.

The 3 Constraints

  1. The "Friday Witness" Rule: You must include at least one specific detail from our Friday in-class activity that cannot be obtained from the readings. (e.g., A specific quote from a peer, a unique moment during the exercise). You must bold this reference.
  2. The "Sensory Audit" Rule: AI is abstract; you are embodied. You must include three distinct sensory details from your observation (e.g., specific text on a flyer, the layout of a room, the noise level). Reflections that remain abstract or generic will not be credited.
  3. The "Artifact" Evidence: Each entry must be accompanied by a photo you took or a sketch you made of the space/object you are analyzing. Your text must explicitly reference a detail visible in this image.

Structure of Your Reflection:

  • Observation: What did you see? (Include your Sensory Details and Artifact reference here).
  • Connection: How does this connect to class concepts? (Include your "Friday Witness" bolded reference here).
  • Reflection: What does this reveal about power/rights in your daily life?

What This Assignment Is (and Is Not)

  • This is an exercise in political thinking, not political advocacy
  • This is about making abstract ideas tangible, not about personal opinion alone
  • This is not a diary, complaint log, or policy proposal
  • This is not about exposing individuals or violating privacy

The emphasis is on analysis, not judgment.

Learning Goals

By completing the Living Rights Portfolio, you will:

  • Learn how global political theories apply beyond states and institutions
  • Develop the ability to observe power in everyday contexts
  • Strengthen analytical writing through regular, low-stakes reflection
  • Understand politics as something that is lived, not just studied

Global politics does not only exist in international organizations, governments, or crises. It exists wherever rules are made, enforced, negotiated, resisted, or taken for granted. This assignment asks you to notice it where you already are.
